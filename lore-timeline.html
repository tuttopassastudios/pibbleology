Lore Timeline

For a condensed table, see Pibble § Timeline. For disputed datings, see Canon disputes.

The Lore Timeline is the agreed-upon (and almost immediately re-disputed) chronological reading of the Pibble archive. It is divided into four eras and one persistent Ongoing condition. Pibbleologists generally hold the timeline to be linear; the Recent Pibble minority holds that it is at least partly backwards. Both readings are reproduced where possible. [1]

Pre-Index

The Pre-Index era contains all material believed to predate the formal archive. As the archive itself has no founding date, the boundary is conventionally drawn at the moment someone first attempted alphabetical order.

Scroll Zero

Scroll Zero is the earliest cited fragment. It opens mid-sentence, with Pibble already speaking, and ends mid-sentence, with Pibble still speaking. Only Pibble's lines are recorded; the responses, if any, were never written down.

Three sub-fragments are preserved: 3a (concerning the temperature of an unordered soup), 3b (the famous reference to I have always been here), and 3c (an extended complaint about a draft from a window that, in context, has not yet been installed). Together they form the textual basis for the Always-Was Doctrine. [2]

The Porch Incident

The Porch Incident is the first record in which Pibble appears in a setting witnessed by other figures — Larnice and an unnamed Postman, later assumed to be one of the Two Postmen. Pibble is found seated on a porch that no one remembers building. The porch is constructed, in normal time, three weeks later, by parties who deny having read the fragment.

First Era

The First Era begins with the founding of Threll-Across-Water and ends with the single afternoon known as The Great Quiet, in which the entire region is reported to have made no sound between approximately one and four o'clock. No witnesses agree on the cause; all agree it was restful.

Pibble is recorded as both absent and present at the founding of Threll, which is treated by contributors not as a contradiction but as a property of Pibble. Oboe of Threll, by contrast, is firmly recorded as elsewhere, despite being the founder.

Middle Era

The Middle Era is dominated by the Spoon Cycle, a sequence of fragments concerning the loss, pursuit, near-recovery, and eventual mislabelling of the Second Spoon. The Spoon Cycle is widely regarded as the archive's most structurally complete narrative arc, in that it both opens and closes — though several contributors have noted that it closes at a different angle.

The Middle Era also contains the first known instance of the Vaster misplacing itself, an event since canonized as the conclusion of nearly every conflict involving the Vaster. Whether this is good narrative craft or merely a habit of the Vaster's is left to the reader.

Late Era

The Late Era opens with the raising of the Pibblet question and is otherwise defined by an increase in marginal notes, footnotes, and footnotes to footnotes. Many contributors regard the Late Era as the archive's most self-aware period; others regard it as overlong.

A characteristic Late Era fragment is the Concerning Sundays cycle, in which Professor Quoth attempts to deliver a lecture on Pibble's preferences only to discover Pibble already seated in the third row, taking notes.

Ongoing

The Ongoing condition is not properly an era. It consists of the Reading Hour, a recurring period — defined loosely as any hour in which nothing reads anything — and the slow accretion of new fragments, marginalia, letters from Aunt Margery, and the occasional revised opinion.

Editorial note. Datings in this article reflect community convention. Where a fragment is dated to "the second Wednesday of an indeterminate week," contributors are asked to leave the indeterminacy in place.
